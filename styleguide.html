Process Analysis: Beep

Video Game / January - April 2019 / Academic Graduation Project

The Project

Beep is a 3D adventure game where the player controls a round ball that can morph into a cube. The player must evade enemies, retrieve their stolen spaceship, and escape a hostile planet.

This project is a revision of a game initially created in 2017 for a game design course.

As a member of the art team, my responsibilities included creating concept art, modelling and texturing 3D assets, and animation cutscenes and characters.

Preparation

The first task we had to complete was a review of our previous game. Due to to time constraints and our own team’s skill level back in 2017, we felt that our resulting game was rushed and flawed, and not quite fulfilling the potential we felt it had. There were a variety of bugs, inconsistencies with level design, empty looking environments, and an incomplete narrative.

Having identified the problems with our original game, we then worked on additional research and conceptualization. I worked on creating general concept art for new assets and visuals, and worked with the rest of the team on brainstorming a narrative that would have a satisfying conclusion for the player. Concept art was made with the idea that we wanted to emphasize the square shapes and rigidity of the game world while contrasting with the round and warm-colour palette of the player character.

Modelling and Animation

Using our concept art as a base, I used proper 3D modelling workflows to create and animate character and enemy models. We also made sure to stick to strict visual styles and colour palettes to define the player character and make them stand out in contrast to the game world: the main character used reds, pinks, and round shapes, while cube enemies and the game world were made up of blues, greens, purples, and cubes and rectangles.

Below are some of the new animations I created for our cube enemy.

Cutscenes

I was also responsible for creating the opening and ending cutscenes of the game to communicate the narrative. We wanted to create an interesting opening and give the player a satisfying conclusion at the end. I started with storyboards to figure out how to I wanted to frame the crash landing, and then went on to build the cutscenes in Unity using their timeline feature.

Level Visuals

Working with my team I also modelled and created many game assets and then placed them into levels. I worked on defining lighting and colour settings to show the passing of time throughout the game, the evolution of the cube enemy's world, and also reflect the increasing difficulty of gameplay.Level 1 begins with the player crashing into an alien planet's forest early in the morning. In level 3, as night begins to fall, the player suffers a setback in their journey. In this level I darkened the skies and added pink fog and ambient lighting to create a sunset.

Problems and Resolutions

Our main problem with this project was figuring out how to elevate our old game. As a team, we decided on revising the overall themes, completely discarding any old, hacky work, and redoing them using proper workflows.

Mid way through the project, we realized that again we were running into the problem of not having enough time and being too ambitious in how many levels and mechanics we wanted to implement. In order to be able to create a final, polished game, we decided to cut back on our scope, by creating 5 levels instead of 6, and implementing only a few new in-game activities.

Evaluation

Over the course of this semester, our team has learned many valuable lessons about self directing and compartmentalizing a team in order to tackle creating a game with a large scope. For example, we split our team into separate groups: art, and programming, with each member being responsible for tasks such as level design, modelling, or coding of enemy AI. We also made use of collaborative tools such as Slack and SourceTree to effectively manage files and all the moving parts of the project without running into problems. Overall we were able to complete and deliver a game that demonstrates everyone’s hard work and is also a satisfying experience for the player.

Process Analysis: Amorphous

Animated Short / January - April 2016 / Academic

The Project

Amorphous is a 3D animated short about an astronaut on an alien planet and his encounter with a strange creature. This short was created over a period of 4 months for an introductory animation course.

I worked on the narrative concept, modelling and rigging of the main character and a portion of the art assets, and editing together a section of the final film.

Preparation

The design brief for this project was to create a short 3D animated narrative, using Maya. As a team we got together to brainstorm some ideas, and I boarded out a story about an astronaut that meets an alien who turns out to be a very sinister character. We split the short among our 4 teammates by environment, so each member was responsible for a quarter of the final short.

Implementation

I was responsible for modelling and animating the astronaut character and the 3D art assets in my scenes. I also rigged and created walk cycles to save my other team members animating time. After rendering out our frames, I edited together my shots, worked on compiling the whole short together, and added visual effects such as the sky, 2D assets, and lighting.

Problems and Resolutions

The major challenge of working on this project was learning Maya and using it to create an animated short at the same time. As a team we made sure to communicate any tips, shortcuts, and workflows to each other as we learned the ins and outs of the program together.

Working as a team of 4 also presented its unique challenges. We split the animation work based on the 4 different areas the main character travels through, but we also had to make sure that our scenes felt consistent across the whole short. To do this, we created concept art that everyone could refer back to and walk cycles for the characters that everyone would use in their scene. At the end of the project, one member did an editing pass to cut our different shots together and apply extra effects to tie everything together visually.

Evaluation

By being open to suggestions and knowing when to cut or adapt our original ideas, I'm satisfied that we were able to successfully tell a complete story while still being faithful to our initial concept and vision.
